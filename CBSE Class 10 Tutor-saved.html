BALANCING CHEMICAL EQUATIONS

A Comprehensive Guide for Class 10

INTRODUCTION: WHY DO WE BALANCE CHEMICAL EQUATIONS?

A chemical equation represents a chemical reaction using symbols and formulas. We balance it to satisfy the Law of Conservation of Mass, which states that mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. According to this law, the total mass of the elements on the reactant side (left side) must be equal to the total mass of the elements on the product side (right side). This means the number of atoms of each element must remain the same before and after the reaction.

IMPORTANT TECHNICAL TERMS

1. Reactants

These are the starting substances in a chemical reaction, written on the left-hand side of the arrow.

2. Products

These are the new substances formed as a result of the reaction, written on the right-hand side of the arrow.

3. Skeletal Chemical Equation

An unbalanced equation that only lists the formulas of the reactants and products without correct proportions.

4. Coefficients

These are the whole numbers placed in front of a chemical formula (e.g., 2 in 2H₂O) to balance the equation. They represent the number of molecules or atoms involved.

5. Subscripts

These are the small numbers written at the bottom right of an element symbol (e.g., 2 in H₂) indicating the number of atoms of that element within a single molecule. You must NEVER change subscripts while balancing because that would change the identity of the chemical compound itself.

THE HIT AND TRIAL METHOD: A STEP-BY-STEP PROCESS

The simplest way to balance an equation is the Hit and Trial method. We use this method because we try different coefficients until the number of atoms on both sides is equal.

1 Write the skeletal equation.

Let's take the reaction of iron with steam:

Fe + H₂O -> Fe₃O₄ + H₂

2 Compare the number of atoms of each element on both sides.

Left Side (Reactants): Fe = 1, H = 2, O = 1
Right Side (Products): Fe = 3, H = 2, O = 4

3 Pick the compound with the maximum number of atoms.

It is often easiest to start with the compound that has the most atoms. In our example, Fe₃O₄ contains the most atoms (3 Fe and 4 O).

To balance Oxygen: There are 4 oxygen atoms on the right and only 1 on the left. To balance them, we place the coefficient 4 in front of H₂O on the left side.

Fe + 4H₂O -> Fe₃O₄ + H₂

4 Balance the next element (Iron).

There are 3 iron atoms on the right and only 1 on the left. We place the coefficient 3 in front of Fe on the left side.

3Fe + 4H₂O -> Fe₃O₄ + H₂

5 Balance the final element (Hydrogen).

On the left side, we now have 8 hydrogen atoms (4 molecules of H₂O, each with 2 atoms: 4 x 2 = 8). On the right side, we have only 2 hydrogen atoms in H₂. To make them 8, we place the coefficient 4 in front of H₂.

3Fe + 4H₂O -> Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂

6 Check the correctness.

Count the atoms again to ensure they are the same on both sides.

Left Side: Fe = 3, H = 8, O = 4
Right Side: Fe = 3, H = 8, O = 4

The equation is now balanced.

7 Representing physical states.

To make the equation more informative, we add symbols in parentheses: (s) for solid, (l) for liquid, (g) for gas, and (aq) for aqueous (dissolved in water).

3Fe(s) + 4H₂O(g) -> Fe₃O₄(s) + 4H₂(g)

Note: H₂O is marked (g) here because it is in the form of steam.

10 PROBABLE QUESTIONS

(For Periodic Test, Class 10 NCERT Standards)

Multiple Choice Questions (1 Mark Each)

1. Which of the following represents the correct balanced equation for the reaction of magnesium with oxygen?

A. Mg + O₂ -> MgO

B. 2Mg + O₂ -> 2MgO

C. Mg + 2O₂ -> MgO₂

D. 2Mg + 2O -> 2MgO

2. To balance the following equation: N₂ + H₂ -> NH₃, the coefficients in front of N₂, H₂, and NH₃ should be:

A. 1, 1, 1

B. 1, 3, 2

C. 2, 3, 2

D. 1, 2, 3

3. What is the physical state of water when it is labeled as (aq) in a chemical equation?

A. It is in the form of steam.

B. It is the solvent in which a substance is dissolved.

C. It is purely in the liquid state.

D. It is in the form of ice.

Short Answer Questions (2 to 3 Marks Each)

4. State the Law of Conservation of Mass and explain its significance in balancing a chemical equation.

Answer involves stating the law and its direct relation to atom count in equations.

5. Balance the following chemical equations:

(i) HNO₃ + Ca(OH)₂ -> Ca(NO₃)₂ + H₂O

Ans: 2HNO₃ + Ca(OH)₂ -> Ca(NO₃)₂ + 2H₂O

(ii) NaOH + H₂SO₄ -> Na₂SO₄ + H₂O

Ans: 2NaOH + H₂SO₄ -> Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O

6. Write the balanced chemical equation representing the reaction of Lead (II) nitrate with Potassium iodide to produce Lead iodide and Potassium nitrate.

Reactants: Pb(NO₃)₂ and KI. Products: PbI₂ and KNO₃.

Ans: Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) -> PbI₂(s) + 2KNO₃(aq)

7. Why can we not balance a chemical equation by changing the subscripts of the reactants or products? Explain with an example.

Answer explains that changing subscripts changes the chemical identity (e.g., H₂O vs H₂O₂), which is not allowed.

8. Translate the following statement into a balanced chemical equation: Barium chloride reacts with aluminum sulfate to give aluminum chloride and a precipitate of barium sulfate.

Reactants: BaCl₂ and Al₂(SO₄)₃. Products: AlCl₃ and BaSO₄.

Ans: 3BaCl₂(aq) + Al₂(SO₄)₃(aq) -> 2AlCl₃(aq) + 3BaSO₄(s)

9. Balance the following and mention physical states: Methane (CH₄) gas burns in the presence of oxygen gas to produce carbon dioxide gas and water vapor.

Ans: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) -> CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g)

10. Identify the reactants and products in the reaction: Calcium oxide + Water -> Calcium hydroxide. Also, write its balanced chemical equation mentioning physical states.

Reactants: CaO, H₂O. Product: Ca(OH)₂.

Ans: CaO(s) + H₂O(l) -> Ca(OH)₂(aq)

This concludes the explanation and the list of probable test questions for Class 10 Chemistry.

Remember to always double-check the final count of atoms on both sides carefully!
